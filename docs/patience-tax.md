# The Patience Tax

*Design document — proposes a delayed-settlement escrow that funds traversal-weighted attribution and the negation layer. Supports the Network Public §3 and §4 separations. Requesting external feedback.*

## Context

Coinless JAR establishes that the base layer doesn't need a token to operate, and that validator revenue arises naturally as a portfolio of service-layer payments under congestion. Genesis 2.0 establishes how to recognise contributions of arbitrary types in a single global ranking.

What's missing is a mechanism that connects the two: when revenue is generated by a service running on JAR, **how does some of that revenue flow back to the contributors who made the service possible** — the dataset providers, the upstream code authors, the reviewers, the deliberative cohorts who recognised the work?

The Network Public thesis names this mechanism: a **patience tax** — a small, fixed share of revenue held in escrow for a fixed window, then distributed by traversal to upstream contributors and by deliberation to public-good purposes.

It is called a "tax" because it is mandatory, applied at the substrate. It is called "patience" because it deliberately delays settlement, so the network can learn from the pattern of traversal across the window before disbursing.

It is also, less obviously, a **narrative-sovereignty mechanism**. The patience tax keeps service revenue inside the contributor loop that produced the substrate the service runs on; without it, revenue exits the network and the contributors most central to a service's outputs depend on exogenous funding to keep contributing. Exogenous funding is the entry point through which other decentralised networks have lost narrative sovereignty — once contributors are paid by external interests, the network's self-account drifts toward whatever those interests want to fund. The patience tax closes that entry point at the substrate level: revenue from a service stays attached to the contributors whose work the service traversed, and to the deliberative cohorts the network has chosen to fund its self-narration.

## Design

### What is taxed

Two revenue streams enter the patience tax:

- **Inference revenue.** Payments to services that produce inferences over data the network ingests (models, indices, derived datasets).
- **Core-time revenue.** Payments to validators for prioritised core-time during congestion, denominated in service-layer tokens (per Coinless JAR).

Free-tier transactions (the steady state under non-congestion) are not taxed: there is no revenue to tax.

### Rate

Initial proposal: **3% of inference revenue, 5% of congestion core-time revenue**. The asymmetry reflects that core-time congestion is the more contested resource and warrants slightly more public-good redistribution.

The rate is constitutional. It can be adjusted by the same mechanism that adjusts other constitutional parameters (a separate process, out of scope here). It cannot be set by a single cohort or service.

### Window

The patience-tax window is a **rolling 12-month period**. Revenue earned at slot *s* enters escrow at slot *s* and disburses at slot *s + 12 months*. The window is constitutional and strictly longer than the negation-layer cohort term (`docs/negation-layer.md`).

While in escrow, funds are held in a protocol-controlled account. They are not lent, not staked, not invested. They sit. The point of the window is not yield — it is **to learn**.

### What disburses, and how

At settlement (end of window), the escrowed amount for that slot's revenue is split into two slices:

- **Traversal slice (proposed: 70%).** Allocated by upstream attribution: the contributors whose data, code, or judgement was traversed during the *production* of the inference receive a share proportional to traversal frequency. This is the mechanism described in Network Public §3 (inference ownership via traversal).
- **Deliberative slice (proposed: 30%).** Allocated by the negation layer. Cohorts (`docs/negation-layer.md`) decide which proposals — foundational dataset recognition, public-good infrastructure, refusal endorsements, retraction honouring — receive funds from this slice.

The 70/30 split is constitutional. Higher traversal share funds upstream creators; higher deliberative share funds public-good and refusal capacity.

### Why a delay

Immediate settlement gives nothing up. Delayed settlement learns from the *pattern* of traversal across the window:

- A piece of upstream work that is traversed once and never again is rewarded for that single traversal. Fine.
- A piece of upstream work that is traversed thousands of times across the window is rewarded for the cumulative pattern, not for the first traversal alone.
- A piece of upstream work that turns out to be flawed and is retracted partway through the window has its share reallocated by the cohort active at retraction time.

Without the window, the network has to decide all of this at the moment of revenue, with information that is necessarily incomplete.

## State Machine

```
                    ┌──────────────┐
   revenue at slot s ──► ESCROW(s) │ (12-month window)
                    └──────┬───────┘
                           │ settle at slot s + window
                           ▼
                    ┌──────────────┐
                    │ disbursement │
                    └──┬────────┬──┘
                       │ 70%    │ 30%
                       ▼        ▼
              traversal slice  deliberative slice
              (auto allocation) (cohort allocation)
                       │        │
                       ▼        ▼
                 upstream     foundational/
                 contributors public-good/
                              refusal
```

Per-slot escrows let revenue from different slots disburse on staggered schedules. There is no batching: revenue earned today disburses on the slot 12 months from today, in isolation from other slots' revenue.

## Traversal Allocation

The traversal slice allocates by **upstream attribution graph**:

- Each inference, when produced, emits a `traversalGraph`: a content-addressed structure naming the upstream contributions used.
- Traversals accumulate during the window: each ingestion of a new traversal graph updates the running totals for upstream contributors.
- At settlement, the escrowed amount is allocated proportional to weighted-traversal frequency.

The traversal graph commitment is content-addressed and stored on-chain (or anchored on-chain via a Merkle commitment to off-chain logs for high-throughput services — see open question 4 in `docs/network-public.md`).

### Anti-gaming

A service could attempt to inflate its own traversal share by self-citing. Three defences:

1. **Self-traversal is not counted.** A service cannot allocate revenue to its own contributors via the traversal slice.
2. **Traversal weight is bounded by ingestion confidence.** A traversal of a contribution with low PoI confidence (per Genesis 2.0's BT lower bound) is weighted less. This prevents inflating fresh, sparsely-bridged contributions.
3. **Cohort review.** The negation-layer cohort can flag suspect traversal patterns; flagging triggers a refusal-of-disbursement vote (>66% cohort threshold) that reroutes the disputed share to the deliberative slice.

## Deliberative Allocation

The deliberative slice flows to the negation-layer cohort active at settlement time. Cohort decisions are made via ranked comparison among candidate allocations and aggregated using Genesis 1.0's weighted lower-quantile. The cohort's decisions are themselves signed events, ingested via Network Public §1.

Settlement of the deliberative slice respects cohort terms: a cohort cannot allocate funds to itself (its members), and a cohort's decision binds only the slice settled during its term.

## JAM Mapping

- **Tax collection.** When a service or validator is paid, a fixed fraction is automatically diverted to the protocol-controlled escrow. **Accumulates.** Implementation: a hook in the service-payment STF transition.
- **Escrow accounting.** A per-slot ledger of escrow balances, indexed by `(slot, revenueType)`. State-modifying. **Accumulates.**
- **Traversal aggregation.** Per-window running totals of weighted-traversal counts per upstream contributor. Heavy aggregation runs in `refine`; final settlement state accumulates.
- **Disbursement.** At slot `s + window`, the escrow at slot `s` is settled. **Accumulates.**

## Lean Invariants

Proposed location: `spec/Jar/PatienceTax.lean`. Properties to prove:

1. **Conservation.** For each slot `s`, the sum of disbursements at slot `s + window` equals the escrow accumulated at slot `s`. No revenue is created or lost.
2. **Window strictness.** Disbursement of escrow from slot `s` happens at slot `s + window` exactly — not earlier, not later.
3. **Self-traversal exclusion.** The traversal slice for service `X`'s inference revenue does not allocate to contributors who are members of service `X`.
4. **Slice-rate bounds.** The traversal slice is `>= 0` and `<= 100%` of the escrow; same for the deliberative slice; sum is `100%`.
5. **Cohort term bounding.** Disbursement of the deliberative slice at slot `t` is decided by the cohort active at slot `t` — strictly not the cohort active at slot `t - window` (when revenue was earned).

## Stub Implementation

A minimal stub for the Lean spec is added at `spec/Jar/PatienceTax.lean` (this PR adds the doc; the stub is added in a follow-up). The stub establishes the types:

```lean
structure EscrowSlot where
  slot : Slot
  revenue : Nat
  revenueType : RevenueType
  traversalGraph : List ContributionRef
  deliberativeSliceRate : Rat -- in [0,1]

structure Disbursement where
  recipient : ContributorRef
  amount : Nat
  source : EscrowSlot
  reason : DisbursementReason -- Traversal | Deliberative
```

Theorems to prove (over a model of the disbursement process):

- `theorem escrow_conservation (s : Slot) : sum_disbursements s = escrow_at s`
- `theorem window_strict (s : Slot) : disburse_slot s = s + window`
- `theorem no_self_traversal ...`

## Open Questions

**1. Rate calibration.** 3%/5% are starting points. Higher rates fund more public-good and upstream attribution; lower rates leave more revenue with services. Modelling against expected revenue scenarios would help.

**2. Window length.** 12 months is long enough to learn but short enough that contributors are not waiting indefinitely for compensation. The right number probably depends on the rate of change in inference patterns.

**3. Slice split (70/30).** Higher traversal share aligns the network closer to a market; higher deliberative share aligns it closer to a public commons. The split encodes the network's character.

**4. Off-chain traversal anchoring.** High-throughput services will not produce per-inference traversal graphs on-chain at full fidelity. Merkle commitments to off-chain logs are the natural compromise. The exact format is worth specifying in a follow-up.

**5. Yield on escrow.** The proposal is that escrow sits idle. An alternative — using escrow for short-term lending or staking — would generate yield but reintroduces token-economic dynamics the coinless thesis specifically rejects. Worth explicitly settling.
